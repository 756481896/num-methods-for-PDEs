Exam info

Xing Cai (xingca at ifi.uio.no)


Four-hour written exam on Thursday Nov 30, 2017, at 9:00-13:00. (Calculator is allowed.)

75 percent of the exam questions will be chosen from a subset of the following six topics:

  • (1) Newton's law of cooling can be described by \[ T^\prime = -k(T-T_s), \] where both \( k>0 \) and \( T_s \) are constants, and initially we have \( T(0)=T_0 \).
    • Find an exact solution \( T(t) \) for Newton's law of cooling shown above.
    • Derive a \( \theta \)-rule for solving this ODE numerically between \( t=0 \) and \( t=t_{\mathrm{end}} \). (Note: \( \theta=0 \) corresponds to the forward Euler scheme, whereas \( \theta=1 \) corresponds to the backward Euler scheme.)
    • Write a Python function cooling(T0, k, T_s, t_end, dt, theta=0.5) to implement the \( \theta \)-rule.
    • How would you verify that your Python function is correctly implemented?
    • For the Crank-Nicolson scheme (\( \theta=0.5 \)), what is the requirement on \( \Delta t \) for avoiding unphysical oscillatory solutions, and why?
  • (2) 1D finite elements for approximation.
    • Use the projection method to approximate the function \( f(x)=x(2âˆ’x) \) on \( [0,1] \) covered by two equal-sized P1 elements. Sketch how the approximate solution looks like. Explain in detail how the computation is done.
    • Repeat the above task but using one P2 element (instead of two P1 elements).
    • Use the collocation method with collocation points \( x=0,0.75,1 \), for the case of two equal-sized P1 elements.
    • Generalize the projection method for approximating a general \( f(x) \) function, with \( N \) equal-sized P1 elements.
  • (3) Solving the 1D wave equation \[ u_{tt} = c^2 u_{xx}, \] \( x\in [0,L],\ t\in (0,T], \) with boundary and initial conditions \( u(0,t) = U_0(t),\quad u_x(L,t)=0,\quad u(x,0)=I(x),\quad u_t(x,0)=V(x)\thinspace . \)
    • Show the details of a numerical scheme that uses finite differences in both time and space.
    • Discuss the stability requirement of the above numerical scheme.
    • Show the details of another numerical scheme that uses finite differences in time but P1 finite elements in space.
    • Compare the two numerical schemes.
    • Sketch one method, using finite differences in time and P1 finite elements in space, for a wave equation with damping \( u_{tt}+b u_{t} = c^2 u_{xx} \).
  • (4) Solving a 1D stationary convection diffusion equation by the finite difference method. More specifically, \[ u_x=\varepsilon u_{xx} \] is to be solved in the domain \( 0 < x < 1 \), where \( \varepsilon>0 \) is a constant and the boundary conditions are \( u(0)=0 \) and \( u(1)=1 \).
    • Show that the above equation has \( u(x)=\frac{1-e^{x/\varepsilon}}{1-e^{1/\varepsilon}} \) as its exact solution.
    • Use centered finite differences to discretize the equation, and set up the resulting linear system. (Assume a uniform mesh that consists of \( N+1 \) points: \( x_0 \), \( x_1 \), \( \ldots \), \( x_N \), where \( x_i=i\cdot h \).)
    • Write a simple Python program that implements the above numerical method and carry out two numerical experiments: (1) \( N=20 \) and \( \varepsilon=0.01 \) and (2) \( N=80 \) and \( \varepsilon=0.01 \).
    • Prove that the analytical solution of the centered finite difference scheme is of form \( u_i=C_1\beta_1^i+C_2\beta_2^i \), where \( \beta_1=1 \) and \( \beta_2=\frac{1+\frac{h}{2\varepsilon}}{1-\frac{h}{2\varepsilon}} \). The values of \( C_1 \) and \( C_2 \) should be determined using the boundary conditions.
    • Derive another numerical scheme where the convection term \( u_x \) is discretized by so-called upwind finite difference. That is, \( u_x \) is approximated at \( x=x_i \) by \[\frac{u_{i}-u_{i-1}}{h}\] Any advantage and/or disadvantage of this numerical scheme when compared with the above scheme based completely on centered finite differencing?
  • (5) Solving the 1D Poisson equation \( -u_{xx}=1 \) with P2 finite elements. The solution domain is \( 0 < x < L \) and the boundary conditions are \( u_x(0)=C \) and \( u(L)=D \), where \( C \) are \( D \) are constants.
    • Derive the variational formulation of the above equation using the Galerkin method.
    • Derive, within a reference element \( X\in[-1,1] \), the three local basis functions \( \tilde \varphi_0(X) \), \( \tilde \varphi_1(X) \) and \( \tilde \varphi_2(X) \).
    • Assuming a uniform mesh with \( N \) elements, calculate the element matrix and vector for each element.
    • Show how to assemble the global matrix and vector.
    • Explain how the boundary conditions are treated.
  • (6) Solving the 2D nonlinear diffusion equation in the unit square \( \varrho u_t = \nabla\cdot (\alpha (u)\nabla u) + f(x,y, t), \) with initial condition \( u(x,y,0)=I(x,y) \) and boundary condition \( \partial u/\partial n=0 \). The coefficient \( \varrho \) is constant and \( \alpha(u) \) is a known function of \( u \).
    • Use the backward Euler method in the temporal direction and centered finite differencing in space. Explain in detail how to set up the nonlinear algebraic equations per time step. (We assume a uniform mesh with \( N_x+1 \) points in the \( x \) direction and \( N_y+1 \) points in the \( y \) direction.)
    • Explain how the Picard method can be used to solve the system of nonlinear algebraic equations.
    • Explain how Newton's method can be used.
    • If the Crank-Nicolson method is used in the temporal direction, which changes are needed in the entire numerical scheme? (Suppose we use Newton's method for solving the resulting nonlinear algebraic equations.)
